"How much will I pay in child support?" It's the first question every parent asks — and in Arizona, the answer isn't a guess. It's a formula.

Arizona uses the **Child Support Guidelines**, a standardized calculation that determines support based on both parents' income, the number of children, and how much time each parent spends with the kids. Here's how it works.

---

## The Basic Formula

Arizona's Guidelines follow this logic:

1. **Start with both parents' gross monthly income** (before taxes)
2. **Make adjustments** (subtract spousal maintenance, other child support obligations)
3. **Combine the adjusted incomes** — this is your "Combined Adjusted Gross Income"
4. **Look up the Basic Child Support Obligation** in the Arizona Schedule based on combined income and number of children
5. **Each parent's share** is proportional to their percentage of combined income
6. **Add adjustments** for medical insurance, childcare, and education costs
7. **Apply the Parenting Time adjustment** based on Table A or Table B
8. **The result** is the monthly child support amount

---

## Key Concepts

### The Arizona Schedule (Line 21)

The Schedule is a lookup table published by the Arizona Supreme Court. It covers combined monthly incomes from $950 to $30,000 and 1 to 6 children.

Example values (1 child):
| Combined Income | Basic Obligation |
|----------------|-----------------|
| $2,000 | $381 |
| $4,000 | $649 |
| $6,000 | $872 |
| $8,000 | $1,069 |
| $10,000 | $1,202 |

If your combined income falls between two listed amounts, the calculator interpolates (calculates the proportional value between the two).

### Table A vs Table B

This is one of the most misunderstood parts of the calculation:

- **Table A** applies when the non-custodial parent has **fewer than 110 overnights** per year with the child
- **Table B** applies when the non-custodial parent has **110 or more overnights**

Table B results in a lower support amount because the non-custodial parent bears more of the direct costs of raising the child during their parenting time.

The 110-night threshold is roughly 30% of the year. Standard visitation (every other weekend plus some holidays) typically falls around 80-90 nights — Table A territory. Equal parenting time (50/50) is 182.5 nights — solidly Table B.

### Proportionate Shares (Line 30)

Each parent's share of the support obligation is based on their percentage of combined income:

**Example**: Father earns $5,000/month, Mother earns $3,000/month
- Combined: $8,000
- Father's percentage: 62.5%
- Mother's percentage: 37.5%

The higher-earning parent pays the difference between their proportionate share and the lower-earning parent's share, adjusted for parenting time.

## What Gets Added to Income?

"Gross income" under Arizona Guidelines includes:
- Wages and salary
- Commissions and bonuses
- Self-employment income (net of business expenses)
- Interest and dividends
- Rental income
- Social Security benefits
- Disability payments
- Unemployment benefits
- Workers' compensation
- Spousal maintenance received

**Not included**: Public assistance (TANF), food stamps, Supplemental Security Income (SSI).

## Common Adjustments

| Adjustment | Effect |
|-----------|--------|
| Spousal maintenance paid | Reduces payor's income |
| Spousal maintenance received | Increases recipient's income |
| Support for other children | Reduces payor's available income |
| Medical insurance premium | Added to basic obligation |
| Childcare costs | Added to basic obligation |
| Extraordinary education costs | Added to basic obligation |
